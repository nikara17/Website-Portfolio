Before You Pick a Degree, Read This

2026 • Nikara Ishwarlall • 7 min read

At eighteen, choosing a degree can feel like choosing a destiny. Application forms open, family opinions in your ear, friends announcing their plans — and beneath it all, the pressure: What if I choose wrong? What if this decision locks me into something that doesn’t fit who I become?

Here is the truth most people don’t say clearly enough: you are not choosing a permanent identity. You are choosing a direction of skill development. And in the world of technology, that distinction matters more than ever.

The illusion of the “right” career

Many matriculants approach career decisions as if there is one correct answer: doctor, lawyer, engineer, accountant, software developer. But the modern economy doesn’t work like a narrow ladder anymore — it works like an ecosystem.

Technology, in particular, is not one job category. It is the infrastructure of almost every industry. Banking, healthcare, logistics, education, entertainment — all of it runs on digital systems. So when you say, “I want to work in tech,” the real question becomes: how do you want to participate in building, improving, or protecting those systems?

You are not choosing a job — you are choosing a problem

Instead of asking, “What job should I pick?” try asking: What kind of problems do I want to solve? That one shift takes you out of fear and into clarity.

Some people are drawn to abstract systems — algorithms, robotics, AI, cybersecurity architectures. Others care about how technology feels in real life: usability, design, communication, and impact. Some are protectors who naturally think about risk, safety, and resilience. Some are interpreters who look at data and see patterns and stories.

When you choose a problem, the pathway becomes clearer — and more personal. It also becomes more flexible, because you’ll realise there are multiple roles that solve similar problems from different angles.

The tech ecosystem is broader than you think

One reason people feel lost is because they think tech equals coding. It doesn’t. Tech careers often fall into different functional layers, each requiring different strengths:

  • Builders: software engineers, robotics engineers, AI developers — they create systems.
  • Translators: product managers, UX designers, business analysts — they connect tech to people.
  • Protectors: cybersecurity analysts, risk specialists — they secure systems.
  • Interpreters: data analysts, researchers — they find meaning and make decisions from data.
  • Enablers: network engineers, cloud/DevOps, IT support — they keep systems running.

Each of these roles is “tech.” Each can lead to global opportunities. And importantly: each has multiple entry points. You don’t need to be the best coder in your class to belong in this world.

Choose skill clusters, not job titles

At eighteen, you are still becoming yourself. Interests deepen. Confidence grows. Exposure changes your direction. So instead of committing to a rigid title, commit to building a cluster of skills that will stay valuable even as roles change.

For example, if you enjoy logical reasoning and structured problem-solving, you may thrive in engineering, systems, AI, or infrastructure. If you enjoy communication, empathy, and design thinking, you may thrive in UX, product, strategy, or digital consulting. If you are detail-oriented and risk-aware, cybersecurity and governance can be a strong fit. If you love patterns and insight, data and analytics could be your lane.

Skills compound. Titles evolve. The person who begins in IT support can grow into cloud architecture. The student who starts in information systems can move into product, security, data, or transformation work.

University is a path — not the only path

A degree can be valuable — it gives structure, credibility, and a strong foundation. But it is not the only route into tech. Certifications, online learning, open-source contribution, internships, and real projects can also build credible pathways. Remote work has widened access even further.

The goal is not to collect credentials. The goal is to build competence. Whether you learn through a university, a college, a bootcamp, or self-study, what matters is your ability to do the work and keep learning.

Ask better questions before you decide

Before you pick a degree, ask questions that reveal alignment:

  • Do I enjoy working with people or systems?
  • Do I prefer structured logic or creative exploration?
  • Do I like building from scratch or improving what exists?
  • Am I motivated by innovation, stability, protection, or transformation?

These questions matter more than prestige. They help you choose a direction that fits your nature — not someone else’s expectations.

Looking forward

The future of work will reward adaptability. Roles will change. Tools will evolve. Entire industries will be reshaped by automation and intelligent systems. What will remain valuable are foundational abilities: critical thinking, communication, ethical awareness, technical literacy, and the courage to learn continuously.

Before you pick a degree, remember this: you are not choosing a lifetime identity. You are choosing where to begin. Choose a direction that builds valuable skills. Choose problems that genuinely interest you. Choose growth over prestige — and trust that your path can evolve as you do.
